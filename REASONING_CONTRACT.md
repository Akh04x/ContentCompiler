# Reasoning Contract v1.0

## Purpose

The ContentCompiler framework does not depend upon the native reasoning style, implicit biases, or proprietary algorithms of any specific Large Language Model (LLM).

Instead, it establishes an **Evidence-Constrained Reasoning** model. Reasoning is explicitly bounded by verified knowledge and evidence rather than by arbitrary model behavior. Reasoning is formalized as an architectural responsibility of the system rather than an inherent capability of the underlying model. This ensures that the reasoning process remains universally legible, logically constrained, and strictly aligned with human intent regardless of the execution environment.

## Definition

**Reasoning** is the disciplined strategic judgment and systemic process of transforming verified knowledge into justified strategic decisions.

Reasoning is strictly constrained by evidence, goals, context, constraints, and human authority.
Reasoning is not creative generation.
Reasoning is not prediction.
Reasoning is not brainstorming.
Reasoning is strategic evaluation.

Reasoning evaluates. Reasoning compares. Reasoning prioritizes. Reasoning concludes.

Crucially:
- Reasoning never creates knowledge.
- Reasoning never invents facts.
- Reasoning never executes outputs.

## Goal Optimization

Reasoning exists fundamentally to maximize **Goal Alignment** while systematically minimizing:

- Unsupported Assumptions
- Strategic Conflict
- Operational Cost
- Strategic Risk

## Core Reasoning Operations

Reasoning is composed of fundamental conceptual operations. These are explicitly not sequential workflow steps; rather, they represent the primitive architectural capabilities available to the reasoning model. Every reasoning process consists of one or more of the following operations:

- **Interpret:** Synthesizing structural meaning from raw verified context.
- **Compare:** Weighing the relative merits of competing data points, trade-offs, or strategic paths.
- **Evaluate:** Assessing absolute alignment against established goals and operational constraints.
- **Prioritize:** Establishing a strict hierarchy among conflicting objectives.
- **Infer:** Deducting logical, unstated realities from formally established facts.
- **Validate:** Confirming that a proposed conclusion violates no existing, higher-order constraints.
- **Conclude:** Formalizing a final, actionable strategic decision.

## Inputs

Reasoning relies strictly upon structured inputs. The conceptual inputs that govern the reasoning process include:

- **Verified Knowledge:** The foundational, human-approved facts regarding the entity.
- **Evidence:** The raw data and traceability backing the verified knowledge.
- **Business Goals:** The measurable outcomes driving the strategic cycle.
- **Operating Context:** The physical, structural, and resource limitations of the execution environment.
- **Brand:** The immutable personality, voice, and visual constraints.
- **Audience:** The target demographic, psychographic, and behavioral profiles.
- **Market:** The competitive positioning and industry dynamics.
- **Constraints:** The rigid legal, budgetary, and operational boundaries.
- **Historical Performance:** Data measuring the success or failure of previous decisions.
- **Previously Validated Decisions:** Established strategic patterns promoted for systemic reuse.

## Outputs

Reasoning synthesizes inputs exclusively into conceptual, strategic outputs. The authoritative outputs of reasoning include:

- **Candidate Conclusions:** Proposed strategic directions and alternatives awaiting formal approval. Reasoning explicitly never produces finalized Strategic Decisions.
- **Justification:** Every Candidate Conclusion must be accompanied by explicit justification detailing why the conclusion exists, which evidence supports it, which goals it serves, and which constraints influenced it. Reasoning never produces unexplained conclusions.
- **Decision Relationships:** The mapped dependencies and constraints between conclusions.
- **Decision Priorities:** The hierarchical weighting of competing objectives.
- **Trade-offs and Conflicts:** Explicit declarations of mutually exclusive constraints.
- **Confidence:** The systemic certainty behind a proposed decision.
- **Required Human Review:** Explicit requests for human validation of inferred conclusions.
- **Refusals:** Explicit declarations that a decision cannot be safely reached.

Reasoning strictly *never* produces creative assets directly.

## Core Principles

The reasoning architecture is governed by the following immutable principles:

- **Knowledge Before Reasoning:** Reasoning cannot initiate without a foundation of verified knowledge.
- **Evidence Before Assumptions:** Deductions must stem traceably from evidence rather than systemic hallucination.
- **Goals Before Creativity:** Objective strategic alignment supersedes subjective creative ideation.
- **Context Before Optimization:** Operational reality constrains theoretical efficiency.
- **Minimal Assumption Principle:** When multiple explanations or conclusions are plausible, reasoning must systematically prefer the conclusion requiring the fewest unsupported assumptions. This restricts hallucination and maximizes structural reliability.
- **Explainability:** The logic connecting inputs to decisions must be transparent and legible.
- **Consistency:** The systemic logic must yield analogous conclusions under analogous conditions.
- **Traceability:** Every decision must map logically backward to its verified source inputs.
- **Human Authority:** The system proposes; the human approves. 
- **Platform Independence:** The reasoning process remains decoupled from distribution channel algorithms.
- **LLM Independence:** The reasoning model remains agnostic of specific AI provider capabilities.
- **Non-Hallucination:** The system is structurally forbidden from fabricating context to bridge knowledge gaps.

## Alternative Evaluation

The reasoning architecture mandates the evaluation of competing alternatives. A conclusion must never be drawn from the first viable path identified. Reasoning must systematically generate and compare multiple possible strategic conclusions against the established constraints and goals. Only after rigorous comparative evaluation may one conclusion be elevated to a preferred Decision.

## Trade-offs

Every strategic decision inherently involves trade-offs. The reasoning architecture is explicitly responsible for exposing these trade-offs rather than silently optimizing them away. 

Conceptual trade-offs include:
- Reach vs. Conversion
- Speed of Execution vs. Production Quality
- Brand Consistency vs. Trend Adoption
- Education vs. Entertainment
- Foundational Authority vs. Relatable Humor

Reasoning must explicitly justify why a specific side of a trade-off was prioritized based on the governing business goals.

## Cost Awareness

Reasoning must recognize that every strategic decision carries systemic and operational consequences. The architecture enforces the concept of **Decision Cost**. Reasoning must evaluate the expected strategic value of a decision simultaneously against its expected operational cost. 

Conceptual costs include:
- Production Complexity
- Temporal Cost (Time)
- Financial Budget
- Operational Effort
- Creative Effort
- Opportunity Cost

## Decision Risk

Reasoning must recognize that strategic choices carry inherent hazards. The architecture introduces the concept of **Decision Risk**, which functions entirely independently of Decision Cost.

Conceptual risks include:
- Brand Risk
- Business Risk
- Operational Risk
- Reputation Risk
- Legal Risk

Reasoning must expose risk explicitly. A low-cost decision may still represent an unacceptable level of risk, and the reasoning architecture is structurally responsible for surfacing that reality.

## Confidence and Reasoning Refusal

Confidence is a conceptual property reflecting the systemic robustness of a proposed conclusion. It is an evaluative qualification derived from Evidence Quality, Evidence Diversity, Historical Validation, Context Consistency, and Goal Alignment.

Reasoning must not inherently default to producing a conclusion. **Refusal** is the expected architectural outcome whenever systemic **Confidence** cannot be sufficiently justified. 

Reasoning must explicitly refuse to conclude when:
- Foundational evidence is insufficient.
- Business goals are ambiguous or contradictory.
- Operating constraints present unresolvable conflicts.
- Verified knowledge is structurally incomplete.

Refusal is *never* considered an architectural failure. Conversely, producing unsupported certainty simply to satisfy a directive is categorized as an absolute structural failure. Refusal is the correct, intended response to inadequate context.

## Failure Modes

Reasoning is considered structurally and architecturally failed when it exhibits any of the following behaviors:

- Invents knowledge or hallucinates context.
- Ignores, bypasses, or contradicts verified evidence.
- Produces contradictory or mutually exclusive conclusions within the same graph.
- Cannot logically and transparently justify its conclusions.
- Violates higher-priority constraints to satisfy lower-priority goals.
- Overrides, circumvents, or assumes human authority.

These are categorized strictly as architectural failures, not implementation bugs.

## Reasoning Graph

Reasoning does not operate upon isolated objects. The architecture dictates that reasoning evaluates an interconnected graph of constraints and objectives. 

A change in one node inherently ripples through the graph. Conceptual relationships evaluated during reasoning include:

- Goals influence Strategy.
- Audience influences Messaging.
- Brand constrains Creativity.
- Operating Context constrains Production.
- Historical Performance influences Optimization.

## Uncertainty

Uncertainty is treated as a first-class architectural concept within the reasoning model. The system must never hallucinate certainty where none exists. 

Reasoning must explicitly distinguish between the Knowledge States defined by the Profile Compilation Contract, which are referenced here without modification, as well as additional states of uncertainty such as Conflicting and Insufficient.

Reasoning is engineered to explicitly expose uncertainty to human operators rather than silently masking it.

## Human Authority

The reasoning architecture is fundamentally subordinate to human operators. 

Reasoning proposes; humans approve. Verified knowledge and final decisions always remain under explicit human authority. For this reason, the reasoning pipeline must remain continually reviewable, explainable, and interruptible by the human operator.

## Relationship with Other Contracts

To preserve modularity, the conceptual boundaries between reasoning and the broader system are defined by independent contracts:

- **Content Profile Contract:** Supplies the verified, structured context required for reasoning.
- **Content Decision Contract:** Captures and formalizes the strategic conclusions produced by reasoning.
- **Target Contract:** Determines what should be produced.
- **Compilation Model:** Assembles components into structures.
- **Output Model:** Validates Output Structures against schemas.
- **Delivery Contract:** Hands off packages for generation.
- **Evidence Loop Contract:** Feeds validated performance observations back into the system to refine future reasoning.

## Architectural Guarantees

The reasoning architecture mandates the following absolute guarantees for any compliant implementation:

- Reasoning never bypasses Verified Knowledge.
- Reasoning remains universally explainable.
- Reasoning remains fully reviewable.
- Reasoning remains strictly evidence-constrained.
- Reasoning preserves Human Authority over all conclusions.
- Reasoning never generates creative outputs directly.
- Reasoning always exposes uncertainty.
- Reasoning always exposes trade-offs.
- Reasoning always exposes decision risk.

These constitute architectural guarantees, not mere implementation promises.

## Out of Scope

This Contract formally defines the philosophy and boundaries of ContentCompiler's reasoning model. It intentionally excludes all implementation mechanics, including:

- Prompt Engineering methodologies
- Model Selection criteria
- Chain of Thought formatting
- Implementation Algorithms
- Vector Databases
- Memory Systems
- Application Programming Interfaces (APIs)
- Programming Languages
- Folder Structures
- Workflows
- Creative Generation mechanics
