# Delivery Contract v1.0

## Purpose

The Delivery Layer bridges the conceptual framework of ContentCompiler and the physical reality of content distribution. While the Output Layer finalizes the structural blueprint of a deliverable (the Content Package), the Delivery Layer is responsible for its physical execution.

This contract establishes the formal behavioral specification for the Delivery Layer. It ensures that the materialization of content occurs without violating or altering any strategic or structural decisions made upstream.

## Definition

The **Delivery Contract** defines the rules governing the Delivery Layer. The Delivery Layer is responsible for the final materialization, formatting, scheduling, and transmission of Content Packages to their final destinations.

Crucially, the Delivery Layer operates under strict negative boundaries:
- The Delivery Layer never alters strategic intent.
- The Delivery Layer never overrides structural requirements (Output Layer constraints).
- The Delivery Layer never generates new content that wasn't approved upstream.
- The Delivery Layer never performs strategic reasoning.

## Layer Responsibilities

The Delivery Layer is explicitly responsible for:
1. **Platform Abstraction:** Translating the conceptually pure Content Package into the specific technical requirements of a destination Platform (e.g., API payloads, HTML, specific video codecs).
2. **Content Package Materialization:** The physical rendering or assembly of the final deliverable.
3. **Execution & Handoff:** Transmitting the materialized asset to external systems (CMS, social media API, email service provider, or human reviewer).
4. **Signal Capture:** Capturing the immediate outcome of the delivery action (success, failure, or engagement metrics) to pass back into the system.

## Platform Abstraction

A **Platform** within the Delivery Layer is an abstraction representing the conceptual destination. 
- The Delivery Layer maps the generalized Output Structure components to the explicit technical capabilities of a specific Platform.
- If a Platform requires a constraint that conflicts with the Content Package, the Delivery Layer cannot silently alter the content to fix the conflict. It must reject the delivery and surface the architectural conflict.

## Inputs

The Delivery Layer consumes exactly one primary input:
- **Content Packages:** The execution-ready, structurally validated deliverables produced by the Output Layer.

## Outputs

The Delivery Layer produces two primary outputs:
- **Delivery Artifacts:** The materialized, published assets residing on external platforms.
- **Raw Performance Signals:** The observable data generated by the physical deployment (e.g., publish success, error logs, early engagement metrics).

## Handoff to Evidence Layer

The Delivery Layer represents the turnaround point in the ContentCompiler loop. 
- Once a Delivery Artifact is published, it begins generating real-world data.
- The Delivery Layer collects these Raw Performance Signals and hands them off to the Evidence Layer.
- The Evidence Layer is then responsible for validating these signals before they can influence the Knowledge Graph.

## Architectural Guarantees

Any process executing the Delivery Contract guarantees:
- The materialized asset is perfectly traceable back to the Content Package.
- No new strategic or structural decisions are introduced during materialization.
- Platform constraints do not retroactively invalidate upstream reasoning.
- All delivery outcomes (success or failure) are captured and handed off for evidence processing.

## Out of Scope

This specification strictly excludes:
- Implementation of specific CMS or social media APIs.
- The software engineering required for video rendering or image generation.
- Specific scheduling algorithms or queue management systems.
- Database storage for raw performance signals.
